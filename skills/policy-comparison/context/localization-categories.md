# Localization categories

These categories are the controlled labels for comparing WHO source content with local policy material. Use them consistently so results stay comparable across examples, tests, and future evaluations.

## Controlled list

- `Aligned`
- `Partially aligned`
- `Divergent`
- `Missing in local policy`
- `More specific in local policy`
- `More restrictive in local policy`
- `Unclear or requires expert review`

## Category definitions

### Aligned

Use when the local policy has the same meaning as the WHO source, even if the wording differs.

Short example:
WHO says implementation should include follow-up. Local policy also requires follow-up with the same intent.

### Partially aligned

Use when the local policy matches one part of the WHO source but omits, weakens, or changes another part.

Short example:
WHO requires two steps. Local policy includes the first step but says nothing about the second.

### Divergent

Use when the local policy meaningfully conflicts with the WHO source.

Short example:
WHO recommends allowing a workflow under stated conditions, while the local policy prohibits it in those same conditions.

### Missing in local policy

Use when the provided local material contains no corresponding statement for the WHO source content.

Short example:
WHO includes a reporting requirement, but the local excerpt contains no related reporting language.

### More specific in local policy

Use when the local policy adds compatible operational detail without changing the core WHO intent.

Short example:
WHO recommends follow-up, and the local policy adds the exact office, form, or timeframe to use.

### More restrictive in local policy

Use when the local policy narrows eligibility, adds stricter conditions, or limits implementation more than the WHO source.

Short example:
WHO allows a broader group, while the local policy limits the same action to a smaller group.

### Unclear or requires expert review

Use when the relationship cannot be classified confidently from the available text.

Short example:
The terms look similar, but the excerpt is too incomplete to determine whether the meanings truly match.

## When to prefer expert review

Escalate rather than over-interpret when:

- the source excerpt is incomplete;
- terminology may not map cleanly;
- the policy wording is legally or clinically ambiguous;
- multiple categories seem plausible from the same evidence.
