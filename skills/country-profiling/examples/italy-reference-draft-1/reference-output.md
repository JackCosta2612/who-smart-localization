## Executive summary

Italy has a universal, tax-funded National Health Service, the Servizio Sanitario Nazionale (SSN), with national stewardship and strong regional responsibility for planning and delivery. The European Observatory's 2022 review and summary describe a system in which central government sets the national benefits package and allocates resources, while regions organize and deliver services locally. This makes regional implementation capacity and variation central to any later policy analysis.

Italy's health outcomes are strong in European context, but the profile sources consistently flag demographic ageing, noncommunicable diseases, regional differences, service delivery pressure, workforce and infrastructure constraints, and the need to strengthen digital health and community care. Immunization is one relevant downstream area because national prevention and vaccination plans exist, but this profile does not compare Italian vaccination policy with WHO guidance.

The profile is sufficient for a first contextual scoping pass for later policy comparison. It is not sufficient for detailed policy comparison until the relevant national policy, implementation, coverage, and regional source classes are reviewed for the chosen topic.

## Country context snapshot

Italy is a high-income EU Member State with a highly regionalized health system. The central government sets national stewardship functions and the national benefits package, while regions finance, plan, and provide services through regional health systems. This central-regional split is a core implementation constraint for later policy analysis because national policy may be interpreted and operationalized differently across regions.

The reviewed sources repeatedly identify demographic ageing as a major pressure on health and long-term care demand. For a profile intended to support SMART Guidelines localization, this means service models, workforce capacity, community care, chronic disease management, and digital continuity of care are relevant background systems to document before comparing any disease-specific policy.

## Population health overview

The European Observatory review states that Italians can expect long lives and reports life expectancy of 83.6 years in 2019, with a temporary fall in 2020 associated with COVID-19 mortality. The same review identifies cardiovascular diseases and cancer as leading causes of death, with infectious respiratory disorders becoming prominent in 2020.

The 2025 Country Health Profile should be treated as the preferred current overview for health status, determinants, accessibility, resilience, and pharmaceutical policy context. Exact current statistics should be extracted directly from that profile or its linked data tables during final profile production rather than copied from this draft without verification.

## Main health issues and burden

The main burden pattern described by the European Observatory is typical of high-income ageing populations: noncommunicable diseases, especially cardiovascular diseases and cancer, dominate mortality. Behavioral risk factors, including tobacco, alcohol, diet, and related lifestyle determinants, are identified as important contributors to morbidity and mortality in the 2022 health system review.

Infectious disease remains relevant for prevention and preparedness. The National Prevention Plan 2020-2025 includes infectious disease priorities, and the PNPV 2023-2025 is an important downstream source class for vaccination strategy. This profile does not assess whether Italian vaccination policies align with WHO recommendations.

## Health system organization and capacity

Italy's SSN provides universal coverage to citizens and legal residents. The European Observatory sources describe a system in which national authorities set the essential benefits package and overall stewardship, while regions are responsible for service organization and delivery. Local health authorities, public and accredited providers, general practitioners, specialists, hospitals, prevention departments, and regional structures are therefore key implementation actors.

The 2022 health system review and summary identify reform priorities including prevention, hospital network restructuring, benefits package updates, performance monitoring, strengthening primary and community care, modernizing infrastructure and equipment, improving digital infrastructure, and workforce training. These themes should shape later policy comparison questions, especially where national policy text assumes implementation capacity that varies by region.

## Healthcare access and coverage

The SSN's universal coverage model is a strong contextual feature. The European Observatory summary states that the SSN provides universal coverage to citizens and legal residents and that the central government defines the national benefits package. The access analysis should still distinguish legal entitlement from realized access, because regional variation, waiting times, workforce availability, and service delivery capacity may affect practical access.

For later policy comparison, eligibility and benefit-package sources should be reviewed for the specific health area. In immunization, the Ministry of Health vaccination entitlement page and the national vaccination calendar are likely necessary source classes, but they should be used later to compare policy content, not in this country profile.

## Sanitary conditions and environmental health

The current reviewed source set is stronger on health system organization than on WASH and environmental exposures. Italy's National Prevention Plan 2020-2025 includes environment, climate, occupational health, injuries, infectious diseases, and NCD prevention among public health priorities, but this draft does not include a detailed WASH or environmental risk dataset.

For a final country profile, add an explicit source on air pollution, climate and health risk, water and sanitation, waste management, or environmental burden if the downstream SMART Guidelines topic depends on environmental or sanitary conditions. Until then, this section should remain cautious and gap-marked.

## Health financing and affordability

The European Observatory health system summary reports that health expenditure was 8.7% of GDP in 2019 and that public sources accounted for 74% of total health spending in 2019, with later pandemic-related increases. These figures should be updated from the 2025 Country Health Profile, OECD data, WHO Global Health Expenditure Database, or national accounts before use in a final quantitative profile.

For policy comparison, financing matters because national entitlements may be affected by regional budget constraints, out-of-pocket spending, waiting times, or service availability. The reviewed sources support identifying financing and affordability as important context, but detailed household financial protection and programme funding evidence should be retrieved for the selected policy area.

## Health workforce, infrastructure, and supply availability

The European Observatory review flags historic underinvestment in the workforce, outdated infrastructure and equipment, and information infrastructure needs as major system challenges. The 2025 Country Health Profile and Health at a Glance: Europe 2024 provide current comparative context on workforce pressure and ageing-related demand.

A final profile should avoid unsourced claims about exact workforce densities, regional staffing gaps, facility counts, medicine shortages, or vaccine supply availability unless extracted from an authoritative source. For later immunization comparison, vaccine procurement, distribution, cold chain, local vaccination points, and regional capacity should be treated as follow-up source classes.

## Digital health and health information systems

Italy is investing in the Fascicolo Sanitario Elettronico 2.0 and wider PNRR Mission 6 digital health infrastructure. Ministry and PNRR materials describe the FSE as a national digital record and data infrastructure effort, including clinical documents and vaccination information among the data classes that can be represented.

The implementation issue for later SMART Guidelines localization is not only whether a national digital health system exists, but whether relevant data are complete, interoperable, consistently used across regions, and available for the specific workflow. This draft does not validate FSE implementation performance or interoperability.

## Equity, vulnerable groups, and regional variation

Regional variation is one of the most important interpretive features of the Italian system. The European Observatory sources identify marked regional differences in health indicators and a decentralized delivery model. The PNPV and ISS summary emphasize equity in vaccination access and harmonization of strategies across the country, which signals that regional differences are relevant for immunization.

Equity analysis should also consider older people, people with chronic conditions, migrants, people in remote or underserved areas, and people affected by socioeconomic barriers. This draft does not include a dedicated equity dataset, so detailed subgroup claims require follow-up source review.

## Current concerns, risks, and watchpoints

Key watchpoints for later localization work are demographic pressure, workforce constraints, infrastructure modernization, regional variation, primary and community care strengthening, digital health implementation, and long-term financial sustainability. These are supported by the European Observatory sources, the 2025 Country Health Profile, Health at a Glance: Europe 2024, and PNRR digital health materials.

For immunization as a later topic, watchpoints include regional implementation consistency, vaccination coverage by cohort and region, public trust and communication, pharmacovigilance, vaccine availability, and the operational role of prevention departments, general practitioners, specialists, pharmacies, and local health authorities. These should be documented later from topic-specific sources.
