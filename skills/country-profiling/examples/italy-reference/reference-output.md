## Executive summary

Italy has a universal National Health Service, the Servizio Sanitario Nazionale (SSN), with national stewardship and regional responsibility for service organization and delivery. The central-regional split is the main implementation fact to preserve for later policy work: national policy text, regional implementation, service capacity, data systems, and coverage evidence should be kept separate.

The 2025 Country Health Profile provides the current high-level picture: Italy has very high life expectancy in the EU context, an ageing population, major noncommunicable disease burden from cardiovascular disease and cancer, access pressure linked to waiting times and out-of-pocket use, workforce constraints, and ongoing resilience and modernization needs. Observatory sources provide the deeper health-system explanation: universal coverage, tax funding, regional delivery, public and accredited private providers, primary care gatekeeping, prevention functions, and reform priorities.

Immunization is a suitable first downstream topic because Italy has official PNPV 2023-2025 and national calendar source material, ISS explanatory pages, AIFA regulatory context, and relevant digital-health source classes. This profile is ready for policy-comparison scoping only. It is not ready for alignment findings until the official attachments, regional implementation evidence, latest coverage datasets, legal/entitlement material, reporting specifications, WHO/SMART inputs, and expert review are assembled.

## Country context snapshot

Italy is a high-income EU Member State with a universal but regionalized SSN. Observatory sources describe national stewardship, national benefit definition, and regional responsibility for financing, planning, and service delivery. This structure is favorable for national policy setting but makes implementation evidence essential: a later comparison should not treat national policy text as proof of uniform local practice.

For SMART Guidelines localization, the system context to carry forward is national-regional governance, local health authorities, public and accredited private providers, general practitioners and paediatricians, prevention departments, hospitals, pharmacies where relevant, and digital infrastructure. These actors and systems should be mapped for the selected downstream topic.

## Population health overview

The 2025 Country Health Profile reports a strong current health-status picture, including life expectancy of 84.1 years in 2024 and demographic ageing pressure. The profile also points to unequal access and resilience issues that matter for implementation. The older population is relevant to many downstream topics, including adult immunization, chronic disease management, long-term care, and digital continuity of care.

This example does not attempt a full indicator table. A final profile should extract only the current statistics needed for the intended downstream comparison and cite the source page, table, or dataset used.

## Main health issues and burden

The main burden pattern is consistent across the OECD and Observatory source family: noncommunicable diseases, especially cardiovascular disease and cancer, dominate mortality, while ageing increases demand for chronic care, long-term care, prevention, and service coordination. Infectious disease prevention remains relevant through the National Prevention Plan and the PNPV source family.

For immunization, the missing burden evidence is topic-specific: vaccine-preventable disease epidemiology, outbreak history where relevant, target-population denominators, risk-group evidence, and coverage trends by cohort and region. Do not infer Italian vaccine priorities from WHO guidance or from general health-system context.

## Health system organization and capacity

The SSN provides universal coverage with central stewardship and regional delivery. The detailed Observatory review and updated summary identify reform priorities around prevention, primary and community care, hospital networks, benefits, performance monitoring, workforce training, infrastructure and equipment modernization, and digital infrastructure.

For handoff purposes, the main implementation risk is that a national source may describe a right, plan, or schedule while regional or local systems determine practical delivery. Later policy comparison should therefore collect national text, regional adoption or operational material, and service delivery evidence before making feasibility or alignment claims.

## Healthcare access and coverage

Universal coverage is a major strength, but entitlement should not be conflated with realized access. The 2025 Country Health Profile flags access pressure, including waiting times and private out-of-pocket pathways, while Observatory sources identify regional variation and capacity constraints.

For immunization, the Ministry entitlement/legal source package and official calendar are required before stating exact mandatory, recommended, free, or eligibility rules. In this Country Profiling example, those source classes are identified for handoff rather than interpreted against WHO recommendations.

## Sanitary conditions and environmental health

The National Prevention Plan includes environment, climate, occupational health, injuries, infectious diseases, and NCD prevention. That is enough to flag sanitary and environmental health as a relevant source class, but not enough for detailed WASH, air pollution, climate, waste, housing, vector, food-safety, or occupational exposure claims.

If the downstream WHO/SMART topic depends on environmental exposure, outbreak preparedness, migrant health, travel vaccination, occupational risk, or climate-sensitive disease, add Ministry, ISS, EEA, ECDC, Eurostat, WHO, or national environment-health sources before making substantive claims.

## Health financing and affordability

The SSN is primarily publicly financed, and the Country Health Profile provides current comparative spending and private-expenditure context. Financing still matters for localization because out-of-pocket costs, waiting times, regional budgets, procurement, reimbursement, and programme funding can affect whether a policy is practically implementable.

For immunization, procurement, supply, delivery funding, regional budgets, and reimbursement or entitlement rules remain follow-up source classes. The profile should not infer vaccine availability or affordability from the existence of national policy text alone.

## Health workforce, infrastructure, and supply availability

The OECD and Observatory sources support flagging workforce pressure, infrastructure modernization, and regional capacity as implementation watchpoints. These system-level constraints are relevant to any topic that depends on local service delivery, continuity of care, reporting, outreach, or follow-up.

For immunization, the missing operational evidence includes vaccination service capacity, prevention department staffing, general practitioner and paediatrician roles, pharmacy involvement where relevant, cold chain, procurement, shortages, and regional delivery pathways. AIFA supports regulatory and pharmacovigilance context, not local supply availability.

## Digital health and health information systems

Italy's FSE 2.0 and broader PNRR digital-health work are relevant to SMART localization because data capture, interoperability, and regional adoption can affect whether digital recommendations are implementable. For immunization, the important downstream questions are vaccination registries, coverage reporting, pharmacovigilance flows, FSE vaccination data, data dictionaries, interoperability, and regional completeness.

This example treats FSE material as a candidate source class because a detailed digital data-flow review has not been completed. Later comparison should request registry specifications, reporting forms, implementation dashboards, and regional adoption evidence before making data-readiness claims.

## Equity, vulnerable groups, and regional variation

Regional variation is the main equity and implementation issue documented by the general health-system sources. The PNPV source family also frames harmonization and equitable access as important, making region, residence, socioeconomic status, legal status, age, clinical risk, and local service access relevant dimensions for later review.

For immunization, likely groups for source review include children and adolescents, older adults, pregnant people where relevant, people with chronic or immunocompromising conditions, healthcare workers, occupationally exposed workers, migrants, unaccompanied foreign minors, travelers, and people in underserved areas. The official calendar and legal sources must be reviewed before stating exact Italian recommendations for these groups.

## Current concerns, risks, and watchpoints

Carry forward these system watchpoints: regional implementation variation, ageing-related demand, NCD burden, access pressure, waiting times, workforce constraints, infrastructure modernization, digital-health adoption, data completeness, financial sustainability, and expert-review needs.

For immunization, carry forward these topic watchpoints as retrieval tasks rather than conclusions: PNPV/calendar updates, NITAG-related processes, Ministry circulars, regional adoption timing, coverage by vaccine/cohort/region, safety surveillance, public communication, supply continuity, and registry/reporting workflows.
